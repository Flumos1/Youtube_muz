> There was already a second couple in the band — the keyboard player and singer, Christine McVie, and the bassist, John McVie. Husband and wife.

> At the same time, the McVies were getting divorced. A husband and wife who now had to sit in the same room, every single day, and make music together.

## BEAT 3 — The Pressure Cooker — 3:30–5:15

> The producers, Ken Caillat and Richard Dashut, weren't really engineers anymore. They were referees in a daily war.
> And to keep the whole thing from grinding to a halt, a black velvet bag of cocaine sat under the mixing desk. Shared. When someone needed a hit, they'd just start humming a tune — that was the signal.
> There was so much of it that the band half-seriously talked about thanking their dealer in the album credits.

> And this is where the story turns into legend.
> They didn't hide the pain. They turned it into songs — and then made each other perform them.
> Buckingham wrote "Go Your Own Way" — a furious, direct shot at the breakup with Stevie. And Stevie sang backing vocals on it. About herself.
> She answered with her own song, "Dreams" — written down the hall in an empty studio once built by the funk legend Sly Stone: black and red walls, a velvet bed in the middle of the room. Quiet revenge, against his rage.
> And Christine wrote "You Make Loving Fun" — about her affair with the band's lighting director. Her husband, John, played bass on the recording. She told him the song was about her dog.

> You'd think it couldn't get worse. But by the end of that year, the one person keeping the band glued together — Mick Fleetwood — and Stevie Nicks began a secret affair.
