> This is the story of *Never Mind the Bollocks, Here's the Sex Pistols* — an album that spent exactly one week being recorded, got its band dropped by two labels before it was even finished, and became the most controversial record in British history.

## BEAT 1 — The Architect (~0:55–2:30)

> His name was Malcolm McLaren. And he wasn't a music manager. He was an art school dropout obsessed with a French idea called Situationism — the theory that you could use spectacle and provocation to expose how fake and hollow mainstream culture really was.
> He ran a clothing shop on the King's Road in London that sold bondage trousers and T-shirts designed to offend. And one day in 1975, he decided a band would be a much more effective weapon than a shirt.
> He didn't find musicians. He found *faces*. Kids who looked right, who had the right kind of anger. John Lydon — who'd later call himself Johnny Rotten — walked in wearing a Pink Floyd T-shirt with "I Hate" written above the band's name in ballpoint pen. McLaren handed him the microphone.
> Lydon couldn't sing. He didn't know any of the songs. He performed to a jukebox in the back of the shop, miming and snarling and staring at the ceiling. And McLaren thought: perfect.
> The band already had a guitarist — Steve Jones, who'd been stealing equipment from other bands for years. A drummer. A bassist named Glen Matlock, who was the only one who could actually play. McLaren put them all in a room together and told them to be loud, be offensive, and mean every word of it.
> He had no interest in hit records. He wanted incidents.

*Визуал: лондонский King's Road 1975, пустая витрина бутика, молодёжь на улице, репетиционная комната с голыми лампочками. Suno: chaos theme — нарастающий, угловатый.*

## BEAT 2 — EMI and the Grundy Disaster (~2:30–4:15)

> In October 1976, EMI — one of the biggest record labels in the world — signed the Sex Pistols. The suits thought they were buying into a trend. They had no idea what they'd actually purchased.
> Six weeks later, on a Wednesday night in December, a segment was cut from a TV show at the last minute and the Sex Pistols were booked as a last-second replacement. The host was a man named Bill Grundy. He was bored. He was possibly drunk. And he decided to provoke them.
> What followed was four minutes of live television that changed British culture.
> The band and their friends swore. On air. At prime time. Grundy egged them on, and they obliged, and the director kept the cameras rolling, and somewhere in the control room a producer realized there was absolutely nothing anyone could do to stop it.
> The next morning, the *Daily Mirror* ran the headline: **THE FILTH AND THE FURY.**
> EMI's switchboard was flooded with complaints. A Thames TV technician refused to work on any future programme featuring the band. A coach driver drove his bus into a wall after hearing the interview on the radio — though that one was probably unrelated.
> EMI dropped them three months after signing them. The Pistols kept the £40,000 advance.

## BEAT 3 — A&M: Six Days (~4:15–5:30)

> A&M Records signed them next. Outside Buckingham Palace. At eight in the morning. McLaren had specifically chosen the location — the most establishment address in Britain, for the most anti-establishment band alive.
> Inside the A&M offices afterward, the band destroyed a bathroom.
> Six days later, A&M terminated the contract. They kept the £75,000.
> Two labels. Zero albums released. £115,000 in advances pocketed. McLaren was delighted. This wasn't failure. This was the whole point. Every firing was a front page. Every front page was a new audience.
> A small independent label called Virgin Records, run by a 26-year-old named Richard Branson, decided that where EMI and A&M saw a catastrophe, he saw an opportunity.

*Визуал: Букингемский дворец на рассвете, подписание контракта, разгром офиса, конверты с деньгами складываются стопкой. Suno: насмешливый, ёрнический motif.*

## BEAT 4 — God Save the Queen (~5:30–7:15)

> In May 1977, Queen Elizabeth II celebrated her Silver Jubilee — twenty-five years on the throne. The country was festooned with flags and bunting. And Virgin released a Sex Pistols single.
> It was called "God Save the Queen." The cover showed the monarch with a safety pin through her nose.
> The BBC banned it immediately. ITV banned it. Most high-street chains refused to stock it. A pressing plant refused to press it.
> Virgin's warehouse was raided. Branson was arrested — not for the music, but for displaying the word on a promotional van driving through London.
> And then, on the week of the Jubilee itself, the band hired a boat and played a concert on the River Thames — sailing past the Houses of Parliament. Police boats intercepted them. McLaren and Branson were arrested on the dock. The crowd that had gathered cheered.
> When the charts came out that week, "God Save the Queen" was listed at number two. Number one was Rod Stewart.
> Almost no one believed it. A song that had been banned from the radio, refused by the shops, and actively suppressed — still selling more than almost anything in the country. The official chart compiler later admitted there had been "irregularities." Many in the industry believe it was number one, and the chart was quietly adjusted so the BBC didn't have to acknowledge a banned record at the top of the charts during the Queen's Jubilee.

*Визуал: лодка на Темзе у парламента, полицейский катер, толпа у причала, газетный чарт с подчёркнутой строчкой #2. Suno: defiance — нарастает к кульминации.*

## BEAT 5 — The Bollocks Trial (~7:15–8:30)

> The album finally came out in October 1977. And almost immediately, a Virgin record shop manager in Nottingham named Christopher Seale was prosecuted under the Indecent Advertising Act of 1889.
> His crime: displaying the word "Bollocks" in his shop window.
> Virgin fought it. They brought in a linguistics professor named John Sutherland, who testified under oath that the word "bollocks" derived from the Old English word *beallucas*, meaning a young bull — and had been used for centuries as an informal term for a clergyman's sermon. As in: "that was a load of old bollocks." A term of dismissal. Of irreverence. Perfectly common language.
> The magistrate acquitted Seale. He noted that the word had been used for centuries, and that the prosecution had failed to establish it was indecent by any reasonable standard.
> It was, in its way, a landmark. A judge in a British court had ruled that a word the establishment wanted silenced was, in fact, just a word. The album could be displayed. The album could be sold. The establishment had gone to court — and lost.

*Визуал: зал суда (AI), профессор за кафедрой, судья, витрина магазина, газеты с заголовком о победе. Suno: court tension → release — резкая тишина, потом одна торжествующая нота.*

## CLIMAX (~8:30–9:45)

> The album peaked at number one in the UK. It had taken them over a year — two sackings, one trial, one river arrest, and one nationally televised swearing incident — to get there.
> But here's the thing about *Never Mind the Bollocks* that gets lost in the tabloid story. The music was extraordinary. Steve Jones played nearly every instrument on the record — bass, guitar, even some of the vocals — because by then Sid Vicious had replaced Glen Matlock as bassist, and Sid couldn't actually play. Jones played everything, and the album sounds like a wall of barely controlled fury bearing down on you at full speed.
> Johnny Rotten's voice — nasal, sneering, contemptuous — turned out to be perfectly suited to saying true things that polite society had agreed not to say out loud.
> They lasted exactly one more tour. In January 1978, on the last night of their first and only American tour, Johnny Rotten walked to the microphone at the Winterland Ballroom in San Francisco and said, to a packed house:
> *"Ever get the feeling you've been cheated?"*
> And walked off stage. The band was finished.
> Sid Vicious was dead thirteen months later.

*Визуал: концертный зал 1978, пустая сцена после ухода, газеты с некрологом Сида, обложка альбома крупным планом. Suno: bitter triumph — свелл, затем тишина.*

## OUTRO + CTA (~9:45–10:30)

> The British government spent eighteen months trying to erase the Sex Pistols. Instead, they created the most-documented, most-analyzed, most-influential British band of the decade.
> Every ban was a headline. Every headline was a listener. Every listener was a recruit to the idea that the rules — the rules about what music should sound like, who should make it, what words were allowed — were just that. Rules. Made by people. Breakable.
> The Clash formed because of the Sex Pistols. Joy Division. The Cure. Siouxsie and the Banshees. U2. Every band that rewrote the 1980s drew a direct line back to four nights at a boutique on the King's Road.
> Malcolm McLaren got exactly what he wanted. He just didn't expect anyone to actually listen to the music.
> So let me ask you something. What's a song — or an album — the establishment tried to kill? One that got bigger because they tried to bury it?
> Tell me in the comments. I'll break it down next.

*Визуал: газетные вырезки разлетаются, логотип канала, пустая сцена в луче света. Suno: chaos theme возвращается — затихает до одной ноты.*
