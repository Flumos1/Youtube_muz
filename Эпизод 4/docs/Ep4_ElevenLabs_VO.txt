In 1999, a nineteen-year-old college dropout built something in sixty days that terrified an entire industry worth tens of billions of dollars.

He didn't steal anything. He didn't break in anywhere. He just wrote code.

<break time="0.6s" />

Within eighteen months, seventy million people were using it. The music industry called it piracy. A federal judge shut it down.

And then something strange happened.

<break time="0.8s" />

Killing it made everything worse.

<break time="1.0s" />

This is the story of Napster — the software that shouldn't have worked, built by a kid who couldn't sleep, that rewrote the rules of music, money, and power in ways the industry still hasn't recovered from.

By the end of this, you'll understand why the biggest mistake in music history wasn't piracy. It was the response to it.

<break time="1.0s" />

Shawn Fanning was eighteen years old and couldn't sleep.

It was 1998. His roommate at Northeastern University kept complaining about something: finding good MP3s online was too hard. You had to know the right websites. The links broke constantly. It was a mess.

<break time="0.5s" />

Fanning had an idea. What if instead of storing music on one server — which could be shut down — you let everyone share directly with each other? No central file. No single point of attack. Just millions of computers connected, offering what they had.

He called it Napster. His online handle was Napster — a nickname for his hair.

<break time="0.5s" />

He coded it in sixty days, barely sleeping. When he finally released it, he expected a few hundred people to try it.

Within a year, the number was seventy million.

<break time="0.6s" />

To understand what that meant in 1999 — the entire population of France, downloading and sharing music for free, simultaneously. Songs that cost fifteen dollars on a CD were suddenly available in three minutes. Every album. Every artist. Every song ever recorded.

<break time="0.5s" />

The music industry had just come off its most profitable decade in history. CD sales had exploded through the nineties. Labels were making money at a rate that had never existed before.

And then a teenager in a dorm room switched the lights off.

<break time="1.0s" />

The labels panicked. But not in the way you might expect.

Their first instinct wasn't to adapt. It was to call their lawyers.

<break time="0.5s" />

The Recording Industry Association of America filed suit against Napster in December 1999. But the lawsuit that changed everything came from somewhere else.

Metallica.

<break time="0.6s" />

In April 2000, Lars Ulrich showed up at Napster's offices with thirteen boxes. Inside were the names of 317,000 Napster users who had shared Metallica songs. He demanded they all be banned.

A demo of their song had leaked onto Napster before it was officially released. Radio stations were playing it. Metallica hadn't authorized any of it.

<break time="0.5s" />

The backlash was immediate and brutal. Metallica — who had built their career partly on fans trading bootleg tapes — were now suing their own audience. They became the most hated band in rock overnight. Lars Ulrich was mocked in every magazine, every forum, every late-night show.

<break time="0.5s" />

But Metallica weren't wrong about the principle. The music was theirs. They hadn't been asked. They hadn't been paid.

The problem was the optics. And the timing. And the fact that seventy million people felt the other way.

<break time="0.6s" />

Meanwhile, inside the labels, a different conversation was happening — one that nobody wanted to have publicly.

Napster had approached the major labels in 1999. They'd offered to license the technology, share revenue, make it legal. The labels had a chance to own the future of music distribution.

They said no. They wanted Napster dead.

<break time="1.0s" />

In February 2001, the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals ruled against Napster.

The logic was clear: Napster knew its users were sharing copyrighted music and did nothing to stop it. That made them liable.

<break time="0.5s" />

They were ordered to block all copyrighted material from their network.

Napster tried. They built filters. They tried to comply. But the filters were leaky — users found workarounds almost immediately. The court ruled the filters weren't good enough.

<break time="0.5s" />

In July 2001, Napster went dark.

Seventy million users suddenly had nowhere to go.

<break time="0.8s" />

And here is where the industry made its fatal error.

They assumed that if Napster was gone, people would go back to buying CDs.

<break time="0.6s" />

They didn't.

<break time="0.8s" />

Within months, Kazaa appeared. Then LimeWire. Then BitTorrent — a technology so decentralized it was almost impossible to shut down. The Napster shutdown didn't end piracy. It taught seventy million people that piracy was possible, then scattered them across a dozen networks that were harder to fight.

<break time="0.5s" />

CD sales, which had peaked in 2000, began a collapse that would last fifteen years. By 2010, album sales had fallen sixty percent. The industry had spent millions on lawyers and won a court case — and lost the war completely.

<break time="1.2s" />

There's a document from 2000 that almost nobody talks about.

It's a memo from inside one of the major labels. The writer — an executive — argues that the labels should buy Napster. The price being discussed was two billion dollars.

<break time="0.6s" />

The memo was ignored.

<break time="0.8s" />

Two billion dollars. For the future of the entire music industry.

They said no.

<break time="1.2s" />

While the labels were in court, someone else was paying attention.

Steve Jobs had watched the whole thing carefully. He understood something the labels didn't: people weren't stealing music because they were criminals. They were stealing it because buying it was too hard.

<break time="0.5s" />

CDs cost eighteen dollars. You wanted one song. You had to drive to a store.

Napster had shown that people would spend hours searching for music, downloading it, organizing it — if the music was good enough. They weren't lazy. They weren't cheap. They just wanted it to be easy.

<break time="0.5s" />

In April 2003, Apple launched iTunes.

Ninety-nine cents a song. A thousand songs in your pocket. Legal, fast, simple.

In its first week, a million songs were purchased.

<break time="0.6s" />

The labels, finally desperate enough to deal, had licensed their catalogs to Apple. They thought they were using Jobs to fight piracy. They didn't understand they were handing him control of their distribution.

Within three years, Apple was the largest music retailer in America.

<break time="0.5s" />

The labels had spent four years in court protecting a business model that no longer existed — and handed the replacement to someone outside the industry entirely.

<break time="1.2s" />

Here is the thing nobody in the industry wanted to say out loud.

Napster wasn't a piracy platform. It was a prototype.

<break time="0.6s" />

What Shawn Fanning had built — on-demand access to all recorded music, instantly, from anywhere — was exactly what consumers wanted. Not just some consumers. Seventy million of them. In eighteen months. With zero marketing budget and a product held together with college-student code.

<break time="0.6s" />

The industry looked at those seventy million people and saw thieves.

They should have seen customers.

<break time="0.8s" />

In 2008, a Swedish company called Spotify launched a service built on exactly the same idea Napster had: all the music, on demand, instantly. The difference was the business model. Spotify licensed the content. They paid the labels. They turned the thing Napster had proven people wanted into something legal.

<break time="0.5s" />

Today, Spotify is worth forty billion dollars.

The four major labels that sued Napster into oblivion — they are all Spotify shareholders.

<break time="0.6s" />

They own a piece of the company that is doing, legally, exactly what they spent four years in court trying to destroy.

<break time="0.8s" />

Shawn Fanning saw the future in 1999.

The industry spent four years and hundreds of millions of dollars proving him right.

Napster is gone. The company that bought its name in bankruptcy tried to relaunch it twice. It never came back.

Shawn Fanning went on to build other things. He never quite replicated the lightning in a bottle he'd created at nineteen.

<break time="0.5s" />

But every time you open Spotify, or Apple Music, or YouTube Music — every time you press play and get exactly the song you wanted, instantly, for free or close to it — you are using the future that Napster invented.

<break time="0.6s" />

The music industry didn't kill piracy. They killed Napster.

And in doing so, they accidentally built the world they were trying to prevent.
