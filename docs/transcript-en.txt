Five musicians lock themselves inside a studio by the bay. Two of the couples in that room have just broken up. A third man is quietly losing his marriage. And their job — their only job — is to make an album about love. They can barely stand to look at each other. And that is exactly why they're about to make one of the best-selling records in history.

But here's the part that still doesn't make sense. They started writing songs about each other — to each other's faces. And then they made their exes sing them. Every day. For months. How that didn't tear them apart by the end is one of the strangest stories in rock.

To understand how big this disaster was, you need to know who's in the room. Fleetwood Mac had been around since the late sixties — but the version everyone remembers came together in the mid-seventies. The drummer, Mick Fleetwood, brought in a young couple: a guitarist named Lindsey Buckingham, and a singer named Stevie Nicks. There was already a second couple in the band — the keyboard player and singer, Christine McVie, and the bassist, John McVie. Husband and wife. Their first album with this lineup exploded. Overnight, they were global stars. Sold-out shows. Real money. The perfect picture. There was just one problem. That whole picture was balanced on two couples — and both of them were about to come apart.

The endless touring finished off whatever was still holding together. Buckingham and Nicks — the couple the band's entire new sound was built on — split. For good. At the same time, the McVies were getting divorced. A husband and wife who now had to sit in the same room, every single day, and make music together. And the man holding all of it up — Mick Fleetwood — found out his own wife had left him for one of his close friends. Three relationships. Dying at the same time. Inside one band. Years later, Christine McVie would sum up those sessions in two words: terminal dysfunction.

In early 1976, they locked themselves inside Record Plant studios in Sausalito, just outside San Francisco. They worked around the clock, to exhaustion — partly so they wouldn't have to think about what was happening between them. The producers, Ken Caillat and Richard Dashut, weren't really engineers anymore. They were referees in a daily war. And to keep the whole thing from grinding to a halt, a black velvet bag of cocaine sat under the mixing desk. Shared. When someone needed a hit, they'd just start humming a tune — that was the signal. There was so much of it that the band half-seriously talked about thanking their dealer in the album credits.

And this is where the story turns into legend. They didn't hide the pain. They turned it into songs — and then made each other perform them. Buckingham wrote "Go Your Own Way" — a furious, direct shot at the breakup with Stevie. And Stevie sang backing vocals on it. About herself. She answered with her own song, "Dreams" — written down the hall in an empty studio once built by the funk legend Sly Stone: black and red walls, a velvet bed in the middle of the room. Quiet revenge, against his rage. And Christine wrote "You Make Loving Fun" — about her affair with the band's lighting director. Her husband, John, played bass on the recording. She told him the song was about her dog.

You'd think it couldn't get worse. But by the end of that year, the one person keeping the band glued together — Mick Fleetwood — and Stevie Nicks began a secret affair. The last support beam became one more crack. By then, nobody in the band could quite explain who was with whom — or why they were all still in the same room.

On February 4th, 1977, the album came out. They called it Rumours. And instead of destroying the band, the pain started to sell. People all over the world heard their own breakups inside those songs. Around forty million copies. One of the best-selling albums ever made. Album of the Year at the Grammys. They literally recorded their own divorce onto tape — and the world bought it forty million times.

Here's the cruelest part of the whole thing. The album that should have ended them made them immortal. But the wounds were real. Sometimes the best thing a person ever makes is born at the exact moment their life falls apart. So tell me — what album do you think came out of real pain?
